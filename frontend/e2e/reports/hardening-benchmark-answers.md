Balances up to BRL 500,000.00 are exempt and remunerated by the Selic rate, calculated based on the end-of-day balance capped by the requirement (p. 1).

### Q21: What authority does the President have to modify tariff rates under the Trade Act of 1974?
**Status**: OK | **Latency**: 17674ms | **Length**: 993ch | **Sources**: 1

Under the Trade Act of 1974, the President has the authority to modify tariff rates by proclaiming modifications or continuances of existing duties, duty-free, or excise treatment, or by imposing additional duties (p. 436). This authority is exercised when the President determines such actions are required or appropriate to carry out trade agreements (p. 436). Specifically, Section 101(a) of the Trade Act of 1974 (19 U.S.C. 2111(a)) grants this power, particularly when existing duties and import restrictions unduly burden U.S. foreign trade and when modifications would promote the Act's purposes (p. 1). There are limitations on this authority:
- **Decrease Limitation** The President cannot decrease a rate of duty to a rate below 40% of the rate that existed on January 1, 1975 (p. 436). - Exception to Decrease Limitation This 40% limitation does not apply if the rate of duty on January 1, 1975, was not more than 5% ad valorem (or its ad valorem equivalent) (19 U.S.C. 2111(b)(2)).

### Q25: How does the Act define and address injury caused to domestic industries by import competition?
**Status**: OK | **Latency**: 15609ms | **Length**: 1409ch | **Sources**: 1

The Trade Act of 1974 defines injury caused to domestic industries by import competition as "serious injury" to a domestic industry producing an article that is like, or directly competitive with, the imported article (p. 2251). This serious injury must be a "substantial cause" (p. 2251). The term "contributing importantly" in this context means an important cause, though not necessarily the most important cause, of the serious injury or threat thereof caused by imports (p. 2252). The Act addresses this injury through several mechanisms:
- **Investigations** The Commission initiates investigations to determine if serious injury (or threat thereof) is substantially caused by imports (p. 2251, p. 2252). - Affirmative Determinations If an investigation under Chapter 1 of Title II (19 U.S.C. 2251 et seq.) results in an affirmative determination of serious injury, relief can be provided (p. 2251). - Import Relief The President can provide import relief, which is considered action taken under Chapter 1 of Title II of the Act (19 U.S.C. 2251 et seq.) (p. 2133). - Provisional Relief Provisional relief can be requested as if a petition had been filed under Section 202(a) of the Trade Act of 1974 (19 U.S.C. 2252(a)) (p. 2252). - Causes of Injury The Act specifically notes that serious injury can result from the reduction or elimination of duties provided under trade agreements (p. 2112, p. 2252).
